Sora, History and awkward growing pains

In the world of artificial intelligence, few tools have generated as much awe and visceral pushback as Sora, OpenAI’s text-to-video model. Since its first preview in early 2024, Sora has evolved from a laboratory marvel into a public social platform, leaving a trail of industry panic, viral memes, and deep-seated ethical debates in its wake.

A Brief History: From "GPT-1 Moment" to Sora 2

The history of Sora is often described in technical "epochs."

  • February 15, 2024 (The Preview): OpenAI stunned the world by releasing HD clips of a woman walking through Tokyo. It was hailed as the "GPT-1 moment" for video—proof that video could be generated from text.
  • December 9, 2024 (Initial Public Release): Released to Plus and Pro users, Sora struggled with physics; famously, basketballs would disappear through hoops or people would grow extra limbs mid-stride.
  • September 30, 2025 (Sora 2): OpenAI launched Sora 2, the "GPT-3.5 moment." This version introduced stable physics and a social app where users could remix videos and use "Cameos."

Popular Moments: The Viral and the Weird

The internet quickly gravitated toward the surreal with the Sora 2 release:

  • The "Stephen Hawking" Memes: Viral clips of Hawking in "Robot Wars" style battles or doing skateboarding tricks became massive hits on TikTok.
  • The Jake Paul Controversy: Satirical videos of Paul in "effeminate clothing" led to legal threats, highlighting the realism of these deepfakes.
  • The "Liar’s Dividend": Realistic clips, like a fake cat fight, led to the phenomenon where real footage is doubted because AI fakes are indistinguishable.

Controversies: "Forgiveness Over Permission"

  1. The Hollywood Reckoning: Tyler Perry halted an $800 million studio expansion after seeing Sora. The MPA and CAA have accused OpenAI of stealing intellectual property.
  2. The MLK Crisis: After disrespectful depictions of Dr. Martin Luther King Jr., OpenAI was forced to implement a reactive "opt-out" policy for historical estates.
  3. The "Watermark" War: Mandatory watermarks were introduced, but third-party tools to remove them appeared within seven days, rendering the safety feature largely ineffective.

Better or Worse?

To supporters, Sora democratizes filmmaking. To critics—like those at Vox who dubbed it an "unholy abomination"—it represents the end of human-led creativity. One thing remains certain: the "sky" (the meaning of Sora in Japanese) is the new battlefield for digital ethics.
